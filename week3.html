Machine Instructions

Coding is keying in specific instructions for a machine that has no prior knowledge other than what is already given to it.
Thus, to show this in practice as well as teach us to be specific in our instructions, we had a machine instructions practice.
Each of us drew out a simple image, wrote out instructions on how to recreate said image, and passed the instructions to
someone else for them to figure out the drawing only with text-based instructions.


My instructions for a matcha bowl and whisk:


  1. Draw a rectangle vertically
  2. Draw lines out from the bottom of the rectangle in the shape of a triangle
  3. Draw an oval around the bottom half of the lines
  4. Draw a curve that connects one end of the oval to the other from the bottom
  5. Colour in the bottom of the lines

I exchanged my instructions with Peran's:


  1. Draw 2 circles, the same size, that are horizontally separated by 2 circle distance
  2. Draw a rectangle thats bottom aligns with the middle of the two circles and overlaps.
    The width of the rectangle is slightly wider than the distance between the 2 circles
  3. Complete the drawing by drawing a smaller rectangle that aligns with the right of the big rectangle.
    Half the height of the big rectangle.

We were supposed to follow the instructions exactly, instead of trying to figure out it out using our own guesses.
However, as humans, both Peran and I did try to guess what the other party was trying to give instructions on.
His instructions were so much more detailed than mine, and I could recreate his design exactly even without
trying to guess what it was, as seen in the image. On the other hand, he had a hard time with mine, and even
assuming it was a pen with a cup. This exercise was helpful in showing me that I had to be more specific in
my instructions. I assumed that my instructions were clear enough, but it was only due to the bias I had in
my head, knowing what I wanted to draw. If it is already so difficult for a human to recreate my instructions,
what more can I expect from a machine?

Making a Face

We were tasked to create a self-portrait of ourselves using simple shapes for homework this week. As someone with a background
in fine arts during high school, simplifying complicated images is difficult for me. Shading and providing natural details has always
come more naturally to me than creating cartoon-like images. Even when transitioning into this degree, it was hard to understand
vectors and shapes. Thus, this piece of homework was quite difficult.

We tried an activity similar to the machine instructions for our self-portrait. We hand-drew what our design might look like when
converted to pixels. Of course, it ended up looking slightly different when I tried to recreate it on Processing. However, I still
felt like it provided a good gauge of where to place the facial features, compared to if I had done it without a sketch.

There was not much information online on how to code a face, and if there was, I didn't feel that it suited my design aesthetics.
Finally, I managed to find a designer's works that I liked. She made use of unique simple shapes that I would not have otherwise
thought of, such as diamonds for the eyes and large blocks of solid colours to frame the character's hair. While it seems rather
simple upon first glance, after understanding how much goes on when doing this from scratch with JS, I thought her code would
be good to reference for my work.

        let darkBrown
        let lightYellow
    
    
        function setup() {
            createCanvas(windowWidth, windowHeight);
            background(255, 255, 255);
            darkBrown = color(71, 13, 0)
            lightYellow = color(255, 349, 196);
            }
    
        function draw() {
            //reference: https://ems.andrew.cmu.edu/2018_60212f/category/02/ (possible ideas)
            //rect(x1, y1, w, h, corners)
            //ellipse(cx, cy, w, h)
            //arc(cx, cy, w, h,)
            
        push();
            rectMode(CENTER);
    
            // back hair
            fill (darkBrown);
            strokeWeight(0);
            rect(width/2, height/2, 230, 400, 90);
    
            //face
            fill (lightYellow);
            strokeWeight(0);
            ellipse (width/2, height/2-60, 200, 200);
      
            //bangs
            fill (darkBrown);
            arc(width/2, height/2-60, 230, 200, PI, TWO_PI);
      
            //left eye
            fill (darkBrown);
            strokeWeight(0);
            ellipse (width/2-50, height/2-35, 10, 10);
      
            //right eye
            fill (darkBrown);
            strokeWeight(0);
            ellipse (width/2+50, height/2-35, 10, 10);
        pop();
      
        push();
            //left eyebrow
            noFill();
            translate(-7.5, -5);
            strokeWeight(2);
            stroke (darkBrown);
            bezier(width/2-50, height/2-35, width/2-45, height/2-40, width/2-40, height/2-40, width/2-35, height/2-35);
            //bezier(400, 400, 410, 390, 420, 390, 430, 400); not responsive
            //(x1, y1, x2, y2, x3, y3, x4, y4)
        pop();
      
        push();
            //right eyebrow
            noFill();
            translate(93, -5);
            strokeWeight(2);
            stroke (darkBrown);
            bezier(width/2-50, height/2-35, width/2-45, height/2-40, width/2-40, height/2-40, width/2-35, height/2-35);
        pop();
      
        push();
            //smile
            noFill();
            strokeWeight(2);
            stroke(darkBrown);
            translate(-20, 0);
            bezier(width/2, height/2, width/2+10, height/2+10, width/2+30, height/2+10, width/2+40, height/2);
        }
