Today's class heavily focused on traditional conceptualising. In the first half of class, we learnt more about
the context and history for vintage games. In the second half, we did a revision on HTML and CSS, as well as
publishing our first repository.

Vintage Games

For our group experiment, my team (Jarrett, Karen, Tisha, Nicole, and I) decided to work with Pacman for our
practice paper prototype. This whole concept of paper prototyping was still very foreign to us, so we chose a
game that was relatively simple. I did not know how we could “animate” the pellets disappearing when Pacman
eats them. However, one of our group members came up with the idea of cutting slits in the paper so that we
could move the strips of paper when players interact with the prototype. Due to a time crunch, we also stuck
some of the ghosts onto the strips, to imitate them chasing Pacman. However, we realised quickly that this
meant they would be very limited in movement, and players could only move Pacman specific direction.

Looking at other groups' works, it might be better to not limit elements on the paper prototype to slits in
the paper. The other groups put their elements on individual paper strips, to allow for easier movement.
I might implement that idea into my final paper prototype, instead of the slits that my group used.
